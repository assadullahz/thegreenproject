Waste & Water Management

Waste & Water Management

Proper waste and water management are crucial for public health, environmental protection, and sustainable development. In Indonesia, the situation demands immediate attention:

Waste Generation

Indonesia generates a staggering 92,960 tons of waste daily (2022 data). Limited infrastructure and overflowing landfills lead to open dumping and burning, causing air and water pollution.

Waste Management: Striving for Improvement

The government recognizes the urgency and has implemented initiatives

National Plan
The 2008 Solid Waste Management Act and President Regulation No. 97/2017 set ambitious goals to reduce waste by 30% and improve handling to 70% by 2025. This focuses on waste reduction, increasing recycling and composting (currently at a low of 13%), and diverting waste from landfills.

Challenges Remain
Despite these efforts, challenges persist. Open dumping persists, and existing landfills are nearing capacity. Additionally, informal waste collection systems often lack proper infrastructure and regulation.

Water Management: Bridging the Gap

While Indonesia boasts abundant water resources, access to clean water and proper sanitation remains a concern:

Limited Access: Although access to improved water sources is around 92%, millions still lack reliable clean water.

Sanitation Issues: Only 86% have access to improved sanitation, with a mere 2% connected to a centralized sewerage system. This means a significant amount of wastewater goes untreated, polluting rivers and groundwater.

Non Revenue Water (NRW): A Hidden Challenge

NRW refers to water lost in distribution networks due to leaks, meter inaccuracies, and illegal connections. In Indonesia, NRW can reach up to 30%, meaning a significant portion of treated water never reaches paying customers.
